# Landmark Decisions in French and European Law

Organized by domain. Each entry includes: citation, facts summary, rule established, and significance for legal practice.

---

## I. Civil Law (Droit civil)

### 1. Jand'heur
**Citation:** Cass. ch. réunies, 13 févr. 1930 (no pourvoi number in modern format; predates current system)
**Published:** S. 1930.1.121, concl. Matter; D. 1930.1.57, rapport Le Marc'Hadour

**Facts:** A child was injured by a truck driven by an employee of the defendant company. The lower court had required proof of a fault in the operation of the vehicle.

**Rule established:** Article 1384 alinéa 1 of the old Code civil (now art. 1242 al. 1) creates a presumption of liability for the custodian (gardien) of a thing that causes damage. The presumption is of fault but also of causation; only force majeure or the victim's own fault can rebut it. The nature of the thing (whether it is motorized or not) is irrelevant.

**Significance:** Foundational case for the entire regime of strict liability for things (responsabilité du fait des choses). Eliminated the requirement to prove a specific defect in the thing. Still the leading authority, even after the 2016 reform (art. 1242).

---

### 2. Desmares
**Citation:** Cass. civ. 2e, 21 juil. 1982, n° 81-12.850
**Published:** Bull. civ. II, n° 111

**Facts:** A pedestrian was hit by a car. The driver argued that the victim's contributory negligence should reduce or eliminate liability.

**Rule established:** The fault of the victim cannot partially exonerate the custodian of a thing under the strict liability regime — only total exoneration for exclusive fault of the victim is admissible. Contributory negligence operating as partial defense was rejected.

**Significance:** Created an anomaly that was quickly corrected by the legislature: the Badinter Law (loi n° 85-677 of 5 July 1985) on road accident compensation was enacted in direct response, establishing a specific statutory regime for traffic accidents. Desmares was then explicitly limited to non-traffic contexts.

---

### 3. Perruche
**Citation:** Cass. ass. plén., 17 nov. 2000, n° 99-13.701
**Published:** Bull. ass. plén., n° 9; D. 2001.332, rapport Sargos

**Facts:** A child was born with severe disabilities. His mother had contracted rubella during pregnancy, but her doctor and a laboratory failed to diagnose it, depriving her of the possibility of requesting a therapeutic abortion.

**Rule established:** The child born with a disability caused by a medical error that deprived his mother of the right to choose abortion can claim compensation for the prejudice resulting from his disability (préjudice de naissance or "wrongful life").

**Significance:** Deeply controversial: the court recognized a claim for damages based on the fact of being born (wrongful life claim). Provoked immediate legislative reversal by loi n° 2002-303 of 4 March 2002 (loi Kouchner / loi anti-Perruche), which prohibits wrongful life claims and channels compensation through a national solidarity fund (ONIAM).

---

### 4. Chronopost
**Citation:** Cass. com., 22 oct. 1996, n° 93-18.632
**Published:** Bull. civ. IV, n° 261; D. 1997.121, note Sériaux

**Facts:** Chronopost (express delivery company) failed to deliver a contract bid on time due to its own logistics failure. Its standard terms contained a clause limiting liability to a refund of the postage price.

**Rule established:** A contractual clause that contradicts the core obligation (obligation essentielle) of the contract must be deemed unwritten (réputée non écrite). A carrier's limitation of liability to refund of postage, when its core obligation is guaranteed timely delivery, empties that obligation of its substance and is therefore void.

**Significance:** First major application of the doctrine of clauses contradicting the obligation essentielle. Subsequently codified by the 2016 reform of contract law in art. 1170 Code civil, which expressly nullifies clauses that "deprive of its substance the debtor's essential obligation."

---

### 5. Baldus
**Citation:** Cass. civ. 1re, 3 mai 2000, n° 98-11.381
**Published:** Bull. civ. I, n° 131; D. 2000.879, note Chauvel

**Facts:** A seller sold 50 photographs at a low price, unaware that the buyer was a dealer who knew they were worth far more. The seller sought annulment of the sale for dolus by omission (réticence dolosive), arguing the buyer should have disclosed the true value.

**Rule established:** There is no general pre-contractual duty to disclose information about the value of the thing being purchased. The buyer's silence about the value of the goods does not constitute dolus. The seller bore the risk of his own ignorance of the market value.

**Significance:** Drew the line between legitimate information asymmetry (exploiting superior knowledge of value) and unlawful deceit. This position was partially reversed by art. 1137 al. 2 of the reformed Code civil (2016), which provides that concealment of information that is determinative of consent can constitute dol if there was a pre-contractual duty to disclose it.

---

### 6. Blieck
**Citation:** Cass. ass. plén., 29 mars 1991, n° 89-15.231
**Published:** Bull. ass. plén., n° 1; D. 1991.324, rapport Chartier

**Facts:** A mentally disabled person living in a specialized care association set fire to a forest. The owner of the forest claimed compensation from the association.

**Rule established:** Liability for the acts of others under art. 1384 al. 1 (now art. 1242) extends beyond the cases expressly enumerated (parents/children, employers/employees) to any person or organization that has accepted the task of organizing, directing, and controlling the life of another on a permanent basis.

**Significance:** Dramatically expanded vicarious liability. Created the general principle of liability for persons under one's authority (responsabilité générale du fait d'autrui), subsequently applied to associations, social workers, sports clubs, etc.

---

### 7. Société Myr'Ho / Costedoat
**Citation:** Cass. ass. plén., 25 févr. 2000, n° 97-17.378
**Published:** Bull. ass. plén., n° 2; D. 2000.673, rapp. Kessous

**Facts:** An employee-pilot negligently destroyed crops while carrying out a task ordered by his employer. The victims sued the employee personally.

**Rule established:** An employee who acts within the limits of the mission assigned by his employer does not personally incur liability toward third parties for damage he causes. The employer absorbs the liability.

**Significance:** Established the immunity of the employee acting within the scope of employment (immunité de l'agent), protecting employees from personal suits when acting within their assigned tasks. A major shift toward concentration of tort liability in the employer.

---

### 8. Faurecia
**Citation:** Cass. com., 29 juin 2010, n° 09-11.841
**Published:** Bull. civ. IV, n° 115

**Facts:** Faurecia, an automobile equipment manufacturer, contracted with Oracle for an IT system that was never properly delivered. Oracle invoked a limitation-of-liability clause capping damages at the amount of fees paid.

**Rule established:** A limitation-of-liability clause is valid between professionals, even in the context of a breach of a core obligation, provided it does not entirely deprive the essential obligation (obligation essentielle) of its substance. A clause that merely caps damages — without eliminating liability altogether — does not conflict with art. 1170 Code civil (codifying Chronopost).

**Significance:** Clarified the boundary between clauses limitatives (valid) and clauses élusives (void under Chronopost). Essential for IT and commercial contract drafting: a well-calibrated cap on damages survives judicial review.

---

### 9. Boot Shop
**Citation:** Cass. ass. plén., 6 oct. 2006, n° 05-13.255
**Published:** Bull. ass. plén., n° 9

**Facts:** Boot Shop suffered losses due to the non-performance of a contract between its commercial partner and a third-party carrier. Boot Shop sued the carrier in tort, despite not being party to the transport contract.

**Rule established:** A third party to a contract may invoke, on the basis of tort liability (responsabilité délictuelle), a contractual breach committed by one of the parties, provided the breach caused the third party damage. The mere establishment of the contractual fault suffices to characterize the tortious fait générateur.

**Significance:** Resolved the long-standing doctrinal debate on the assimilation of contractual and tortious fault for third-party victims. A contractual breach automatically constitutes a tort vis-à-vis third parties who suffer damage from it.

---

### 10. Bertrand — Responsabilité des parents
**Citation:** Cass. civ. 2e, 19 févr. 1997, n° 94-21.111
**Published:** Bull. civ. II, n° 56

**Facts:** A child playing rugby injured another child during a match. The victim's parents sued the parents of the child who caused the injury.

**Rule established:** Parental liability under art. 1242 al. 4 (then art. 1384 al. 4) is a responsabilité de plein droit. It is engaged by the mere causal involvement (fait causal) of the minor child in the damage, without the need to prove any fault by the child. Only force majeure or the exclusive fault of the victim can exonerate the parents.

**Significance:** Eliminated the requirement to prove the child's fault for parental liability. Strengthened protection of victims of damage caused by minors.

---

### 11. Vice caché — Présomption irréfragable du vendeur professionnel
**Citation:** Cass. civ. 1re, 28 mars 2000, n° 97-18.737
**Published:** Bull. civ. I, n° 101

**Facts:** A defective gas heater caused bodily injury to the buyer. The professional seller claimed ignorance of the defect.

**Rule established:** The professional seller is irrebuttably presumed to have known of the hidden defect (présomption irréfragable de connaissance du vice) under the garantie des vices cachés regime (art. 1641 Code civil). The seller cannot escape liability by claiming ignorance.

**Significance:** Foundational for product liability under the Code civil. Imposed strict liability on professional sellers for hidden defects, reinforcing consumer protection prior to the transposition of the EU Product Liability Directive.

---

### 12. Réforme de la prescription — Application de l'article 2224
**Citation:** Cass. civ. 1re, 11 févr. 2010, n° 09-10.269
**Published:** Bull. civ. I, n° 34

**Facts:** Following the loi n° 2008-561 of 17 June 2008 reforming prescription, a dispute arose over whether the new five-year limitation period (art. 2224 Code civil) applied to claims that had accrued before the reform.

**Rule established:** The new five-year prescription period applies to claims existing at the entry into force of the reform, but the total time elapsed under the old regime is credited. The new period runs from the entry into force of the law (19 June 2008) or from the date the claimant knew or should have known the facts, whichever is later, without exceeding the old period.

**Significance:** Key transitional decision clarifying how the 2008 prescription reform interacts with pre-existing claims. Essential reference for calculating limitation periods in ongoing civil disputes.

---

### 13. Imprévision — Rupture avec Canal de Craponne
**Citation:** Cass. civ. 3e, 6 mars 1876 (Canal de Craponne) — reversed by art. 1195 Code civil (ord. 2016-131)

**Facts:** A canal company sought judicial revision of a fee fixed by contract in the 16th century, arguing that changed economic circumstances made the contract unbalanced.

**Rule established:** (Historical) The Cour de cassation refused to allow courts to revise contracts on grounds of changed circumstances (imprévision), holding that pacta sunt servanda prevailed absolutely. This position was maintained for 140 years.

**Significance:** The 2016 reform of contract law (art. 1195 Code civil) expressly overturned Canal de Craponne by introducing judicial power to revise or terminate contracts affected by unforeseeable changes in circumstances (imprévision/hardship). The contrast between the historical refusal and the modern codification is a key reference point in French contract law.

## II. Criminal Law (Droit pénal)

### 1. Lehideux et Isorni c/ France
**Citation:** CEDH, 23 sept. 1998, n° 24662/94
**Court:** European Court of Human Rights (Cour européenne des droits de l'homme)

**Facts:** Editors of a publication that had placed an advertisement glorifying Marshal Pétain were convicted of apologie de crimes de guerre under French law.

**Rule established:** The conviction violated art. 10 ECHR (freedom of expression). Even speech about historical events touching on war crimes requires that the interference with expression be "necessary in a democratic society." The categorical prohibition without proportionality review was excessive.

**Significance:** Limits French criminal law restrictions on historical speech; requires courts to apply proportionality under art. 10 ECHR, not merely apply the categorical offences in the Code pénal.

---

### 2. Decision on criminal liability of legal persons
**Citation:** Cass. crim., 2 déc. 1997, n° 96-85.484
**Published:** Bull. crim., n° 408

**Facts:** A company was charged with an offence committed by its directing organ (organe ou représentant). The company challenged whether its representative's act could be imputed to it.

**Rule established:** For a legal person to be criminally liable under art. 121-2 Code pénal, the offence must be committed by its organ or representative acting on behalf of the entity. The court clarified the conditions for this imputation.

**Significance:** Confirmed the framework for corporate criminal liability introduced by the 1994 Code pénal; subsequent case law progressively extended imputation to situations where the offence was committed in the course of company activities even without identifying a specific natural person.

---

### 3. Légitime défense — classic statement
**Citation:** Cass. crim., 16 févr. 1967 (reference decision on proportionality in self-defense)

**Rule established:** Légitime défense (art. 122-5 Code pénal) requires: (1) an actual and present attack; (2) a necessary defense; and (3) proportionality between the defense and the seriousness of the attack. An excessive response loses the protection.

**Significance:** Established the three-prong proportionality test that remains the standard framework for assessing self-defense claims.

---

### 4. Nanterre tribunal on "voluntary ignorance" / mens rea in business
**Citation:** Cass. crim., 28 juin 1902 and subsequent doctrine on intention
**Modern restatement:** Art. 121-3 Code pénal (1994)

**Rule established:** Art. 121-3 establishes the hierarchy of mental elements: intentional (intentionnelle), reckless (mise en danger délibérée), and negligent (faute simple — only punishable if expressly provided). For délits, intent is presumed unless the text specifies otherwise.

---

### 5. Garde à vue — QPC
**Citation:** Cons. const., 30 juill. 2010, n° 2010-14/22 QPC
**Published:** JO 31 juill. 2010

**Facts:** Two questions prioritaires de constitutionnalité challenged the garde à vue regime, arguing that the absence of effective access to counsel during questioning violated constitutional rights.

**Rule established:** The existing garde à vue provisions (former arts. 63 et seq. CPP) were declared unconstitutional insofar as they did not guarantee the right to effective assistance of counsel from the outset of custody, in violation of art. 16 DDHC 1789 and the right to a fair trial.

**Significance:** Forced the legislature to reform garde à vue through loi n° 2011-392 of 14 April 2011, guaranteeing counsel's presence during interrogation from the first hour. Watershed moment for constitutional review of criminal procedure.

---

### 6. CEDH — Garde à vue et droit à l'avocat (Salduz)
**Citation:** CEDH, gr. ch., 27 nov. 2008, Salduz c/ Turquie, n° 36391/02
**Court:** European Court of Human Rights (Grande Chambre)

**Facts:** A minor was questioned during police custody without access to a lawyer. His statements were later used as the basis for his conviction.

**Rule established:** Article 6 §§ 1 and 3(c) ECHR require that, as a rule, access to a lawyer should be provided from the first interrogation by police, unless there are compelling reasons to restrict it. Use of statements obtained without access to counsel irretrievably prejudices the right to a fair trial.

**Significance:** The Salduz doctrine directly influenced the French garde à vue reform of 2011. The Cour de cassation relied on this ECHR jurisprudence in Cass. crim., 19 oct. 2010 (n° 10-82.306) to declare the pre-reform garde à vue regime incompatible with ECHR art. 6.

---

### 7. Abus de biens sociaux
**Citation:** Cass. crim., 6 févr. 1997, n° 96-80.615
**Published:** Bull. crim., n° 48

**Facts:** A company director used corporate funds for personal expenses. The question of the prescription starting point arose, as the misuse was concealed in the company's accounts.

**Rule established:** The prescription for abus de biens sociaux (art. L241-3 Code de commerce) runs not from the date of the act, but from the date the offense was discovered — or could have been discovered — by the victim or a party with standing, unless the concealment was deliberate. This applies the theory of infraction dissimulée.

**Significance:** Established the delayed prescription rule for financial offenses involving concealment, allowing prosecution years after the facts. A key authority in white-collar criminal law, frequently applied in corporate fraud and corruption cases.

---

### 8. Cybercriminalité — Accès frauduleux à un STAD
**Citation:** Cass. crim., 20 mai 2015, n° 14-81.336
**Published:** Bull. crim., n° 119

**Facts:** An individual accessed a computer system (STAD) without authorization by exploiting a security vulnerability, arguing that the system was insufficiently protected and therefore implicitly open.

**Rule established:** The offense of fraudulent access to an automated data processing system (art. 323-1 Code pénal) is constituted by the mere fact of accessing or remaining in a system without authorization, regardless of whether the system's security was weak or whether the perpetrator had malicious intent. The absence of robust security measures does not constitute implied authorization.

**Significance:** Confirmed a broad interpretation of art. 323-1 CP, protecting even poorly secured systems. Essential precedent for cybercrime prosecution in France.

---

### 9. Harcèlement moral — Élément constitutif
**Citation:** Cass. crim., 6 déc. 2011, n° 10-82.266
**Published:** Bull. crim., n° 249

**Facts:** An employer subjected an employee to repeated demeaning behavior, excessive workload, and isolation. Criminal proceedings were brought under art. 222-33-2 Code pénal.

**Rule established:** Moral harassment (harcèlement moral) under art. 222-33-2 CP requires repeated acts (agissements répétés) that have the object or effect of degrading working conditions and are likely to affect the victim's rights, dignity, physical or mental health, or career. Neither specific intent (dol spécial) nor a premeditated plan is required — the effects on the victim suffice.

**Significance:** Confirmed that criminal harassment does not require proof of malicious intent, aligning the criminal standard with the labor law definition (art. L1152-1 C. trav.). Strengthened criminal enforcement against workplace harassment.

---

### 10. Non-assistance à personne en danger
**Citation:** Cass. crim., 26 mars 1997, n° 95-83.956
**Published:** Bull. crim., n° 123

**Facts:** A bystander witnessed a person in peril (accident victim) but failed to intervene or call emergency services, despite being able to do so without risk.

**Rule established:** The offense of non-assistance à personne en danger (art. 223-6 al. 2 Code pénal) requires: (1) knowledge of a peril threatening a person; (2) ability to intervene (directly or by calling for help) without risk to oneself or third parties; and (3) voluntary abstention. The obligation applies even when the intervention would only consist of calling emergency services.

**Significance:** Clarified the scope of the duty to assist, confirming that failure to phone for help constitutes the offense. A uniquely French obligation with no equivalent in many common law systems.

---

### 11. Terrorisme — Association de malfaiteurs terroriste
**Citation:** Cass. crim., 29 nov. 2016, n° 16-80.023
**Published:** Bull. crim.

**Facts:** Individuals who had traveled to conflict zones were prosecuted for association de malfaiteurs terroriste (art. 421-2-1 CP) upon return to France, even absent a specific identified planned attack.

**Rule established:** Criminal terrorist association does not require identification of a specific, determined terrorist project. Participation in a group whose purpose is to prepare acts of terrorism suffices. The preparatory character of the offense allows early intervention by criminal authorities.

**Significance:** Broadened the scope of art. 421-2-1 CP as a preventive prosecution tool. Central authority for counter-terrorism strategy, allowing prosecution before a concrete attack is planned.

---

### 12. Récidive et individualisation — Peines plancher (abrogation)
**Citation:** Cons. const., 9 août 2007, n° 2007-554 DC (validation); subsequently loi n° 2014-896 of 15 Aug. 2014 (abrogation)

**Facts:** The loi n° 2007-1198 of 10 August 2007 introduced mandatory minimum sentences (peines plancher) for repeat offenders. The Conseil constitutionnel reviewed the law's constitutionality. It was later repealed in 2014.

**Rule established:** (2007) Peines plancher for récidivistes were declared constitutional, provided the judge retained the power to derogate by reasoned decision. (2014) The legislature repealed the system entirely, returning to full judicial individualization of sentences under art. 132-1 CP.

**Significance:** Illustrates the tension between legislative severity for repeat offenders and the constitutional principle of individualization of sentences. The 2014 repeal confirmed the primacy of judicial discretion in sentencing.

---

### 13. Principe de nécessité des peines — Non bis in idem
**Citation:** Cons. const., 16 juin 1999, n° 99-411 DC
**Published:** JO 19 juin 1999

**Facts:** Constitutional review of a statute providing for cumulative criminal and administrative penalties for the same conduct.

**Rule established:** The principe de nécessité des peines (art. 8 DDHC 1789) requires that penalties be proportionate to the offense. Cumulative criminal and administrative sanctions for the same facts must remain proportionate overall, reflecting the non bis in idem principle.

**Significance:** Constitutionalized the proportionality of penalties and framed the non bis in idem doctrine in French law. Referenced in all subsequent debates on cumulative sanctions (tax/criminal, regulatory/criminal).

---

### 14. Stoïkoff — Surveillance sans contrôle judiciaire
**Citation:** Cass. crim., 14 oct. 2020, n° 19-87.190
**Published:** Bull. crim.

**Facts:** A suspect challenged the legality of surveillance measures conducted during an enquête préliminaire without authorization from the juge des libertés et de la détention (JLD).

**Rule established:** Extended use of coercive investigative measures during a preliminary inquiry without effective judicial oversight may violate arts. 6 and 8 ECHR. Courts must ensure that intrusive measures are subject to adequate judicial control.

**Significance:** Triggered a major legislative reform (loi n° 2021-1729 of 22 December 2021) restructuring supervision of investigative measures and reinforcing the JLD's role. A landmark for the balance between investigative efficiency and fundamental rights.

---

## III. Labor Law (Droit du travail)

### 1. Nikon
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 2 oct. 2001, n° 99-42.942
**Published:** Bull. civ. V, n° 291; D. 2001.3148, note Bitan

**Facts:** An employer searched and read emails stored in a subfolder labeled "personal" on a company computer, discovered during a disciplinary investigation. The employee had been dismissed partly on the basis of those emails.

**Rule established:** The employee retains the right to privacy (droit au respect de la vie privée et du secret des correspondances) even at the workplace. Emails stored in a folder identified as personal cannot be opened by the employer without the employee's presence or consent.

**Significance:** Foundational decision for the right to digital privacy in employment. Subsequently refined: in Cass. soc., 17 mai 2005, n° 03-40.017, the court held that emails sent from a professional account are presumed professional (not personal) unless the employee has labeled them personal.

---

### 2. Brinon
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 31 mai 1956
**Published:** Bull. civ. IV, n° 533

**Facts:** An employer dismissed several employees for economic reasons but had not followed any special procedure, as no statutory procedure for collective economic dismissal yet existed.

**Rule established:** Recognized economic reasons as a valid ground for individual dismissal — establishing that the employer's economic difficulties could constitute a legitimate cause for termination even absent the employee's personal fault.

**Significance:** First major recognition of economic dismissal (licenciement économique) as a legal concept, predating the statutory framework. The modern regime (art. L. 1233-1 et seq. Code du travail) was built on this case-law foundation.

---

### 3. Obligation de sécurité — amiante
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 28 févr. 2002, n° 99-17.221 and four related decisions (les arrêts amiante)
**Published:** Bull. civ. V, n° 81

**Facts:** Former employees of asbestos-manufacturing companies developed occupational diseases from asbestos exposure. They claimed compensation from their employers.

**Rule established:** The employer's obligation of safety (obligation de sécurité) is an obligation de résultat (strict duty): the employer must protect employees from occupational hazards, and any failure — even without personal fault — constitutes an inexcusable fault (faute inexcusable) if the employer had or should have had awareness of the risk.

**Significance:** Transformed the legal nature of the employer's safety obligation from an obligation de moyens to an obligation de résultat. Triggered massive litigation in asbestos cases and reshaped workplace safety law.

---

### 4. Rupture conventionnelle
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 9 mars 2011, n° 10-11.581
**Published:** Bull. civ. V, n° 63

**Facts:** A dispute about the validity of a rupture conventionnelle (negotiated termination) where the employee alleged it had been signed under duress.

**Rule established:** A rupture conventionnelle (art. L. 1237-11 et seq.) is only valid if the mutual agreement is free and informed. The existence of a labor dispute or disciplinary proceedings does not automatically vitiate the consent, but duress or fraud does.

**Significance:** Defined the conditions for challenging a rupture conventionnelle homologuée, balancing the legislator's intent to create a flexible exit mechanism with protection against employer pressure.

---

### 5. Licenciement sans cause réelle et sérieuse — Barème Macron
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 11 mai 2022, n° 21-14.490 and n° 21-15.247 (plenary decisions on the constitutionality of the Barème)
**Published:** Bull. civ. V

**Facts:** Employees challenged the constitutionality and ECHR compatibility of the statutory cap on damages for unfair dismissal (Barème Macron, art. L. 1235-3) introduced by ordonnance n° 2017-1387.

**Rule established:** The Barème Macron is compatible with the French Constitution and with art. 10 of the ILO Convention n° 158 and art. 24 of the European Social Charter (both interpreted as not requiring full compensation).

**Significance:** Ended years of conflicting decisions from lower courts (many of which had set aside the cap on individual basis). Confirmed the capped-damages system as the governing framework for dismissal litigation.

---

### 6. Harcèlement moral — Méthode de gestion
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 29 juin 2011, n° 09-71.107
**Published:** Bull. civ. V

**Facts:** An employee alleged that systematic management practices — excessive pressure, humiliation, and unreasonable objectives — constituted moral harassment, even though applied collectively to the entire team.

**Rule established:** Management methods that impose constant pressure, set unreasonable objectives, or involve demeaning treatment may constitute harcèlement moral under art. L1152-1 Code du travail, even when the methods are collective and not specifically targeted at one individual.

**Significance:** Extended the concept of moral harassment to systemic managerial practices (harcèlement managérial). Employers must review management styles and ensure they do not create a degrading work environment, even when no individual targeting is intended.

---

### 7. Clause de non-concurrence — Contrepartie financière
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 10 juill. 2002, n° 00-45.135 (and Cass. soc., 29 janv. 2003, n° 00-44.781)
**Published:** Bull. civ. V

**Facts:** An employment contract contained a post-termination non-compete clause without any financial compensation for the employee during the restriction period.

**Rule established:** A non-compete clause is null and void unless it provides an adequate contrepartie financière paid to the employee after termination of the employment contract. The financial counterpart must be proportionate to the restriction imposed.

**Significance:** Mandatory validity condition for all non-compete clauses in French labor law. Without financial compensation, the clause is entirely void and unenforceable, regardless of other conditions (geographic/temporal limitations).

---

### 8. Uber — Requalification des travailleurs de plateforme
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 4 mars 2020, n° 19-13.316
**Published:** Bull. civ. V

**Facts:** A VTC driver working through Uber challenged his independent contractor status. Uber set pricing, imposed GPS-tracked routes, and applied ratings-based sanctions including account deactivation.

**Rule established:** When a digital platform unilaterally sets pricing, controls service conditions, and exercises disciplinary power (sanctions for non-compliance), the hallmarks of subordination are established. The relationship must be requalified as an employment contract (contrat de travail).

**Significance:** Landmark gig-economy ruling applying the Société Générale (1996) subordination test to platform workers. Established that algorithmic control can constitute the lien de subordination. Influenced EU platform-worker directive discussions.

---

### 9. Forfait jours — Suivi effectif de la charge de travail
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 13 sept. 2023, n° 22-17.340
**Published:** Bull. civ. V

**Facts:** An employer applied a forfait jours agreement (art. L3121-64 C. trav.) without ensuring regular monitoring of the employee's workload as required by the applicable collective agreement.

**Rule established:** A forfait jours convention is null if the applicable collective agreement lacks sufficient guarantees for monitoring the employee's workload, rest time, and work-life balance — or if the employer fails to implement such guarantees in practice. Upon nullity, the employee is entitled to overtime pay for all hours worked beyond 35 hours per week.

**Significance:** Strict judicial control over forfait jours. Employers must demonstrate active, documented workload-monitoring procedures. Nullity carries heavy financial consequences (retroactive overtime calculation).

---

### 10. Égalité salariale — Discrimination syndicale par comparaison de carrière
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 3 nov. 2011, n° 10-18.036
**Published:** Bull. civ. V

**Facts:** A union delegate claimed career discrimination, showing that his salary and promotion trajectory lagged significantly behind comparable non-union colleagues with similar seniority and qualifications.

**Rule established:** Courts must compare the career trajectory of the union representative with a panel of comparable colleagues (même ancienneté, même qualification, même emploi). A significant unjustified gap creates a presumption of discrimination, shifting the burden to the employer to justify the disparity.

**Significance:** Established the panel comparison method (méthode du panel de comparaison) as the standard tool for proving union discrimination. Widely used in all forms of employment discrimination litigation.

---

### 11. Baby Loup — Laïcité dans le secteur privé
**Citation:** Cass. ass. plén., 25 juin 2014, n° 13-28.369
**Published:** Bull. ass. plén.

**Facts:** A childcare assistant at a private nursery (crèche Baby Loup) was dismissed for wearing a religious veil in violation of the internal rules (règlement intérieur) imposing a neutrality obligation.

**Rule established:** A private employer may restrict religious expression in the workplace through the règlement intérieur, provided the restriction is (1) justified by the nature of the tasks to be performed and the functioning of the enterprise, (2) proportionate to the aim pursued, and (3) not a blanket ban but targeted and reasoned.

**Significance:** Major ruling on laïcité in the private sector. Established that the proportionality test must be applied case-by-case. Influenced the adoption of art. L1321-2-1 Code du travail (loi Travail 2016), which allows internal neutrality clauses under strict conditions.

---

### 12. Air France — Obligation de sécurité de moyens renforcés
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 25 nov. 2015, n° 14-24.444
**Published:** Bull. civ. V

**Facts:** An Air France employee developed an occupational disease linked to asbestos exposure. The employer argued it had taken all precautions available given the state of knowledge at the time.

**Rule established:** The employer's safety obligation is no longer an obligation de résultat (strict liability) as established in the arrêts amiante (2002). The employer can exonerate itself by proving it took all necessary prevention measures prescribed by arts. L4121-1 and L4121-2 Code du travail. The obligation is now one of moyens renforcés (reinforced obligation of means).

**Significance:** Overturned the strict-liability approach to occupational health established by the 2002 asbestos decisions. Rebalanced employer liability while maintaining a high standard of preventive action. Employers must prove affirmative compliance with all nine prevention principles.

---

### 13. Congés payés et maladie — Conformité EU
**Citation:** Cass. ass. plén., 17 nov. 2023, n° 21-20.776
**Published:** Bull. ass. plén.

**Facts:** An employee on long-term non-occupational sick leave claimed accrual of paid leave during illness, relying on EU Directive 2003/88/EC (art. 7, right to annual paid leave).

**Rule established:** Sick leave — whether occupational or not — must count for paid-leave accrual under EU law. French provisions that limited accrual during non-occupational sick leave were set aside as incompatible with EU Directive 2003/88/EC.

**Significance:** Triggered legislative reform: loi n° 2024-364 of 22 April 2024 amended art. L3141-5 Code du travail to provide 2 days/month accrual during non-occupational sick leave (cap 24 days/year). Full 2.5 days/month for occupational illness/accident with no one-year cap. Major alignment of French labor law with EU requirements.

---

### 14. Barème Macron — Validation définitive
**Citation:** Cass. soc., 11 mai 2022, n° 21-14.490 and n° 21-15.247 (avis de l'Assemblée plénière)
**Published:** Bull. civ. V

**Facts:** Employees challenged the constitutionality and ECHR-compatibility of the statutory cap on damages for unfair dismissal (Barème Macron, art. L1235-3 Code du travail), arguing it prevented adequate reparation of harm.

**Rule established:** The Barème Macron is compatible with art. 10 of ILO Convention n° 158 and with art. 24 of the European Social Charter. National courts must apply the statutory scale and may not depart from it on an individual basis.

**Significance:** Ended years of conflicting lower-court decisions — many of which had set aside the cap. Confirmed the capped-damages system as the binding framework for all unfair dismissal litigation in France.

---

## IV. Administrative Law (Droit administratif)

### 1. Blanco
**Citation:** TC, 8 févr. 1873, n° (classic reference, predates modern numbering)
**Published:** Recueil Lebon (D. 1873.3.17)

**Facts:** A young girl was injured by a railway wagon operated by employees of a State tobacco manufacturer. Her father brought a civil action against the State in ordinary courts.

**Rule established:** The liability of the State for damage caused by agents performing a public service is neither governed by the principles of the Civil Code, nor falls under the jurisdiction of ordinary courts. It is subject to special rules and falls under administrative courts (tribunaux administratifs) and the Tribunal des conflits.

**Significance:** The founding decision of French administrative law: established (1) the autonomy of administrative liability from civil law, and (2) the jurisdiction of administrative courts over State liability claims. Still the foundational authority for the separation of powers between administrative and judicial orders.

---

### 2. Cadot
**Citation:** CE, 13 déc. 1889
**Published:** Rec. CE p. 1148

**Facts:** A municipal official challenged his dismissal. The question was whether the Conseil d'État had jurisdiction or whether the matter had to first go to the minister.

**Rule established:** The Conseil d'État is a court of general jurisdiction for administrative disputes; the ministerial theory (ministre-juge) — which required citizens to obtain a prior ministerial decision before going to court — was abandoned.

**Significance:** Established the right of direct access to administrative courts without prior exhaustion of a ministerial recourse. Made the Conseil d'État a true court rather than a mere superior administrative authority.

---

### 3. Dehaene
**Citation:** CE Ass., 7 juil. 1950, n° (Rec. CE p. 426)
**Published:** Rec. CE p. 426; D. 1950.538, note Morange

**Facts:** Civil servants went on strike, and the government challenged whether civil servants had a right to strike at all.

**Rule established:** Civil servants have a constitutional right to strike (recognized by the Preamble of the Constitution of 1946, which forms part of the bloc de constitutionnalité). However, the right is not absolute: the government may restrict or limit it when necessary to ensure continuity of public service (continuité du service public), particularly for services essential to the national interest.

**Significance:** Reconciled the right to strike with the principle of continuity of public service. Established the framework for restricting the right to strike in the public sector, still applied today.

---

### 4. Barel
**Citation:** CE Ass., 28 mai 1954, n° (Rec. CE p. 308)
**Published:** Rec. CE p. 308; S. 1954.3.97, note Rougevin-Baville

**Facts:** Several candidates for the ENA entrance competition were refused admission to the competition without stated reasons. They were suspected of being Communist Party members.

**Rule established:** The equal right of access to public employment and public office (égalité d'accès à la fonction publique) is a general principle of law with constitutional rank. Political opinions cannot serve as a basis for exclusion from a competitive examination unless a specific legal text authorizes it. The administration must justify exclusion.

**Significance:** Established the judicial review of discriminatory exclusions from public service, protecting freedom of opinion in the civil service. A landmark for the principle of equality before the law.

---

### 5. Nicolo
**Citation:** CE Ass., 20 oct. 1989, n° 108243, Nicolo
**Published:** Rec. CE p. 190; RFDA 1989.813, concl. Frydman

**Facts:** A candidate contested the validity of the 1989 European Parliament elections, arguing that allowing French overseas territories to vote was incompatible with an EU treaty.

**Rule established:** The Conseil d'État will review the compatibility of French legislation with EU treaties. Where there is a conflict between a French statute enacted after the treaty and the treaty itself, the treaty prevails (primauté du droit européen in French administrative courts).

**Significance:** Completed the supremacy of EU law in France: previously the Conseil d'État refused to review statutes enacted after treaties. After Nicolo, EU law is fully supreme over all subsequent French legislation in administrative courts (mirroring what the Cour de cassation had held in Cass. ch. mixte, 24 mai 1975, Société des Cafés Jacques Vabre).

---

### 6. Koné
**Citation:** CE Ass., 3 juil. 1996, n° 169219, Koné
**Published:** Rec. CE p. 255; RFDA 1996.870, concl. Frydman

**Facts:** The French government intended to extradite a Malian national. He argued the extradition treaty could not override a fundamental principle recognized by the laws of the Republic (PFRLR).

**Rule established:** The prohibition on extradition for political purposes is a fundamental principle recognized by the laws of the Republic (PFRLR), which has constitutional rank. Such a principle prevails even over an international treaty in French administrative law.

**Significance:** Established a constitutional limit on the supremacy of international law: PFRLRs can override treaty obligations, reflecting French constitutional identity.

---

### 7. Canal — Contrôle des ordonnances article 16
**Citation:** CE Ass., 19 oct. 1962, n° 58502, Canal
**Published:** Rec. CE p. 553

**Facts:** Three OAS leaders were sentenced to death by a special military court (Cour militaire de justice) created by presidential ordonnance under art. 16 of the Constitution (emergency powers). They challenged the legality of the court.

**Rule established:** Ordonnances issued under art. 16 of the Constitution that permanently modify the legal order are justiciable acts subject to judicial review by the Conseil d'État. The creation of an exceptional court lacking procedural guarantees was annulled.

**Significance:** The CE asserted its power of review over acts taken under the most extreme executive power. Rejected the notion that art. 16 ordonnances constitute unreviewable actes de gouvernement when they affect fundamental rights. A critical check on executive authority during states of emergency.

---

### 8. Danthony — Vice de procédure et influence sur la décision
**Citation:** CE Ass., 23 déc. 2011, n° 335033, Danthony
**Published:** Rec. CE

**Facts:** Public servants challenged a decree reorganizing the administration, arguing that a consultative committee had not been properly consulted before its adoption.

**Rule established:** A procedural irregularity (vice de procédure) does not necessarily lead to annulment of the administrative decision. Annulment is warranted only if the irregularity: (1) was liable to influence the outcome (a exercé une influence sur le sens de la décision), or (2) deprived the claimants of a procedural guarantee.

**Significance:** Before Danthony, virtually any procedural flaw could lead to annulment. The decision introduced a proportionate approach: minor procedural defects that could not have changed the outcome are tolerated. Widely applied and cited by lower courts.

---

### 9. Référé-liberté — Commune de Calais (droit d'asile)
**Citation:** CE, ord. réf., 23 nov. 2015, n° 394540, Ministre de l'intérieur c/ Commune de Calais
**Published:** Rec. CE

**Facts:** Migrants living in makeshift camps near Calais faced severe humanitarian conditions. The juge des référés was asked to order emergency measures to protect fundamental rights.

**Rule established:** The right to asylum and the right to dignity constitute libertés fondamentales within the meaning of art. L521-2 CJA. The juge des référés may order the State to take emergency measures (provision of shelter, water, sanitation) when administrative inaction constitutes a grave and manifestly illegal infringement of these rights.

**Significance:** Expanded the scope of référé-liberté to humanitarian crises. Confirmed that the juge des référés can issue positive injunctions compelling the administration to act, not merely to refrain from acting.

---

### 10. Responsabilité hospitalière — Bianchi
**Citation:** CE Ass., 9 avr. 1993, n° 69336, Bianchi
**Published:** Rec. CE p. 127

**Facts:** A patient suffered severe neurological damage following an arteriography performed in a public hospital. No medical fault was established.

**Rule established:** A public hospital may be held liable without fault (responsabilité sans faute) for medical harm when: (1) the medical act was necessary for diagnosis or treatment; (2) the risk was known but exceptional; (3) the patient had no particular predisposition; and (4) the damage is exceptionally severe and bears no relation to the patient's initial condition.

**Significance:** Extended strict administrative liability to medical risks (aléa thérapeutique) in public hospitals. Subsequently codified and broadened by the loi Kouchner (loi n° 2002-303 of 4 March 2002), which created the ONIAM solidarity fund for medical accidents without fault.

---

### 11. Police administrative vs police judiciaire — Baud
**Citation:** TC, 7 juin 1951, n° 01148, Dame Noualek (and TC, 12 juin 1978, Société Le Profil)
**Published:** Rec. TC

**Facts:** Disputes arose over whether acts of law enforcement belonged to police administrative (prevention of public order disturbances) or police judiciaire (detection and investigation of criminal offenses), determining which court had jurisdiction.

**Rule established:** The distinction between police administrative and police judiciaire depends on the purpose (finalité) of the measure: (1) police administrative aims to prevent disturbances to public order (sécurité, salubrité, tranquillité) — jurisdiction lies with administrative courts; (2) police judiciaire aims to detect and prosecute offenses — jurisdiction lies with judicial courts. The purpose is assessed at the moment the contested act was taken.

**Significance:** Foundational distinction for French administrative law. Determines both jurisdiction and the applicable liability regime. Applied daily in contentious cases involving law enforcement actions.

---

### 12. État d'urgence — Contrôle par le CE
**Citation:** CE, ord. réf., 11 déc. 2015, n° 394989 et 394990 (état d'urgence — assignation à résidence)
**Published:** Rec. CE

**Facts:** Following the terrorist attacks of 13 November 2015, the state of emergency was declared. Individuals subject to assignations à résidence challenged the measures before the juge des référés.

**Rule established:** Measures taken under the état d'urgence (loi n° 55-385 of 3 April 1955) are subject to full judicial review by the administrative judge, including in référé-liberté proceedings. The juge des référés verifies whether the measure is necessary, proportionate, and supported by sufficient factual evidence.

**Significance:** Confirmed that état d'urgence does not create a judicial-review-free zone. The CE exercised active oversight, annulling disproportionate measures. Later reinforced when état d'urgence provisions were partly incorporated into permanent law (loi SILT, n° 2017-1510 of 30 October 2017).

---

### 13. Urbanisme — Danthony appliqué aux permis de construire
**Citation:** CE, 14 oct. 2015, n° 390867 (and CE, 22 févr. 2018, n° 395963)
**Published:** Rec. CE

**Facts:** A third party challenged a permis de construire on grounds of procedural irregularity in the consultation process (enquête publique or avis de l'architecte des bâtiments de France).

**Rule established:** Applying the Danthony principle to planning permits: a procedural irregularity affecting a permis de construire leads to annulment only if it was likely to have influenced the decision or deprived a guarantee. Additionally, under art. L600-5-1 Code de l'urbanisme, the judge may suspend the proceedings to allow regularization of a curable defect rather than annulling the permit outright.

**Significance:** Introduced pragmatic flexibility in urbanisme litigation. Reduced the vulnerability of construction permits to purely formal challenges, balancing legal security for builders with the rights of third-party challengers.

---

### 14. Grande-Synthe — Contentieux climatique
**Citation:** CE, 19 nov. 2020, n° 427301, Commune de Grande-Synthe
**Published:** Rec. CE

**Facts:** The commune of Grande-Synthe, a coastal town particularly vulnerable to rising sea levels, challenged the insufficiency of State climate action to meet greenhouse gas reduction commitments under the Paris Agreement and EU Regulation 2018/842.

**Rule established:** A municipality directly affected by climate change has standing to challenge the adequacy of State climate action before administrative courts. The CE ordered the Government to justify, within three months, that its greenhouse gas trajectory was compatible with its commitments.

**Significance:** First use of injunction powers (art. L911-1 CJA) to compel climate compliance by the State. The follow-up decision (CE, 1er juillet 2021) found the State had failed and issued further injunctions with deadlines. Opened climate litigation as a new frontier in French administrative law.

---

### 15. Czabaj — Délai raisonnable de recours
**Citation:** CE Ass., 13 juill. 2016, n° 387763, Czabaj
**Published:** Rec. CE

**Facts:** A retired military officer challenged a pension decision notified without any mention of time limits or remedies. Under art. R421-5 CJA, the two-month appeal period had not started running.

**Rule established:** Even in the absence of proper notification of remedies, the recipient of an administrative decision cannot challenge it indefinitely. A reasonable time limit of one year from notification applies as a backstop, except in exceptional circumstances.

**Significance:** Created the délai raisonnable principle to prevent indefinite legal uncertainty. Extended to implicit decisions (CE, 18 mars 2019) and financial decisions (CE, 31 mars 2017). The Conseil d'État has recommended its codification in the CJA.

---

## V. Digital Law / Data Protection

### 1. CNIL v. Google LLC
**Citation:** CNIL, 21 janv. 2019, délibération n° 2019-001
**Authority:** Commission nationale de l'informatique et des libertés

**Facts:** Complaints were filed by La Quadrature du Net and None Of Your Business (NOYB) after GDPR came into force, alleging that Google's consent mechanism for personalized advertising was unlawful.

**Rule established:** For consent to targeted advertising to be valid under GDPR art. 7 and recital 32, it must be: (1) freely given (not bundled with general terms acceptance); (2) specific (not aggregated across services); (3) informed (privacy information was excessively dispersed and incomplete); (4) unambiguous (pre-ticked boxes are invalid). Google's mechanism failed all four requirements.

**Significance:** First major GDPR sanction in France: €50 million fine (largest at the time in Europe). Established CNIL's interpretation of valid consent for Big Tech advertising models. Note: CNIL had jurisdiction as Google's GDPR establishment was not yet in Ireland, avoiding the one-stop-shop mechanism.

---

### 2. Schrems II
**Citation:** CJUE, 16 juil. 2020, Facebook Ireland and Schrems, aff. C-311/18 (Data Protection Commissioner v. Facebook Ireland Ltd, Maximillian Schrems)
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2020:559

**Facts:** Maximillian Schrems filed a complaint against Facebook Ireland, arguing that his personal data transferred to Facebook's US servers was not adequately protected because US intelligence surveillance laws (FISA Section 702, EO 12333) enabled access incompatible with EU fundamental rights.

**Rule established:** (1) The EU-US Privacy Shield adequacy decision was invalidated as US law did not guarantee equivalent protection to EU law (no proportionality, no effective judicial redress for EU citizens). (2) Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) remain valid in principle, but data exporters must conduct a Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA) to verify whether the destination country's law ensures equivalent protection in practice. If not, transfers must be suspended.

**Significance:** Second invalidation of EU-US transfer mechanism (following Schrems I / Safe Harbor in 2015). Requires all EU controllers to assess US law before transferring data. Triggered a major revision of SCCs (new SCCs issued by EU Commission in June 2021) and ongoing EU-US negotiations (resulting in the EU-US Data Privacy Framework adequacy decision of 10 July 2023).

---

### 3. Planet49
**Citation:** CJUE, 1er oct. 2019, Planet49, aff. C-673/17
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2019:801

**Facts:** Planet49 ran a promotional lottery in Germany where participation required ticking a consent checkbox for advertising cookies. The checkbox was pre-ticked by default. The German consumer protection authority challenged this.

**Rule established:** (1) A pre-ticked checkbox does not constitute valid consent under Directive 95/46/EC and the ePrivacy Directive 2002/58/EC (read in light of GDPR). Consent must be given by a clear affirmative act. (2) The service provider must provide clear information on the duration of the cookies and on third-party access, before obtaining consent.

**Significance:** Settled the "cookie consent" debate: active opt-in is mandatory under EU law. Pre-checked consent boxes are definitively unlawful. Informed the CNIL's major cookie guidelines of 2020 (délibération n° 2020-091) and the subsequent enforcement wave.

---

### 4. Google Spain — Droit à l'oubli
**Citation:** CJUE, 13 mai 2014, Google Spain SL et Google Inc. c/ AEPD et Mario Costeja González, aff. C-131/12
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2014:317

**Facts:** A Spanish citizen requested the delisting of links to newspaper articles mentioning a past real-estate seizure from Google search results. The Spanish data protection authority (AEPD) ordered Google to delist the results.

**Rule established:** The operator of a search engine is a data controller within the meaning of Directive 95/46/EC. Under certain conditions — where the information is inadequate, irrelevant, no longer relevant, or excessive in relation to the purposes of processing — the data subject has the right to request that links to web pages containing personal information be delisted from search results.

**Significance:** Consecrated the "right to be forgotten" (droit à l'oubli) in EU law, later codified in RGPD art. 17. Established the obligation for search engines to process delisting requests. Foundational for the entire body of subsequent case law balancing the right to information against data protection.

---

### 5. CNIL c/ Clearview AI
**Citation:** CNIL, 20 oct. 2022, SAN-2022-019
**Authority:** Commission nationale de l'informatique et des libertés

**Facts:** Clearview AI scraped publicly accessible photographs from the internet to feed a facial recognition database used by law enforcement. The CNIL investigated following complaints from French residents.

**Rule established:** The mass collection (scraping) of biometric data (facial images) from publicly available internet sources cannot be based on the legitimate interest legal basis. Processing biometric data for identification purposes without consent violates RGPD arts. 6 and 9. The CNIL affirmed its extraterritorial jurisdiction over controllers not established in the EU but processing data of EU residents.

**Significance:** €20 million fine. Major precedent on biometric data, facial recognition, and the limits of web scraping under GDPR. Affirmed extraterritorial application of the RGPD.

---

### 6. CNIL c/ Criteo — Publicité ciblée
**Citation:** CNIL, 15 juin 2023, SAN-2023-009
**Authority:** Commission nationale de l'informatique et des libertés

**Facts:** Criteo, a major advertising technology company, deployed cookies for targeted advertising across partner websites. The CNIL investigated the validity of consent obtained, the transparency of information provided, and compliance with data subject rights.

**Rule established:** The adtech company bears the burden of proving that valid consent was obtained before depositing advertising cookies. Consent must be traceable and documented. Inadequate information about data processing purposes and insufficient mechanisms for exercising access and erasure rights constitute separate violations.

**Significance:** €40 million fine — one of the largest CNIL sanctions. Confirmed the strict approach to consent in the programmatic advertising ecosystem. Adtech intermediaries cannot rely on partner websites' consent mechanisms without verifying their validity.

---

### 7. CNIL c/ Amazon France Logistique — Surveillance des salariés
**Citation:** CNIL, 27 déc. 2023, SAN-2023-021
**Authority:** Commission nationale de l'informatique et des libertés

**Facts:** Amazon France Logistique equipped warehouse workers with handheld scanners that measured in real time their activity rates, idle time, and scanning speed. The CNIL investigated the proportionality of this employee monitoring system.

**Rule established:** Continuous real-time monitoring of employee activity through connected devices violates the principles of proportionality and data minimization (RGPD art. 5(1)(c)) when the surveillance goes beyond what is necessary for the stated purposes (logistics management). Measuring idle time to the second and tracking individual productivity in real time constitutes excessive processing.

**Significance:** €32 million fine. Landmark application of GDPR to workplace surveillance and AI-driven management. Established that algorithmic worker monitoring must comply with minimization and proportionality, even when serving legitimate business interests.

---

### 8. Google Analytics — Transferts post-Schrems II
**Citation:** CNIL, 10 févr. 2022, mises en demeure (coordinated with other EU DPAs)
**Authority:** Commission nationale de l'informatique et des libertés

**Facts:** French websites using Google Analytics transferred user data (IP addresses, unique identifiers) to Google servers in the United States. Post-Schrems II, the CNIL assessed whether adequate safeguards existed for these transfers.

**Rule established:** The use of Google Analytics as configured at the time resulted in unlawful transfers of personal data to the United States, as the supplementary measures implemented by Google (encryption, pseudonymization) did not prevent access by US intelligence agencies under FISA Section 702. Website operators are responsible for ensuring that their analytics tools comply with RGPD Chapter V requirements.

**Significance:** Coordinated enforcement action across multiple European DPAs. Forced a revision of standard web analytics practices and accelerated adoption of privacy-compliant alternatives or properly configured proxy solutions.

---

### 9. Fashion ID — Responsabilité conjointe pour les plug-ins sociaux
**Citation:** CJUE, 29 juill. 2019, Fashion ID GmbH & Co. KG c/ Verbraucherzentrale NRW, aff. C-40/17
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2019:629

**Facts:** Fashion ID integrated Facebook's "Like" button on its website, which automatically transmitted visitor data to Facebook upon page load, before any user interaction with the button.

**Rule established:** A website operator that embeds a third-party social media plug-in is a joint controller (responsable conjoint, RGPD art. 26) with the social network for the collection and transmission of data, but not for the subsequent processing by the third party. Valid consent must be obtained before the plug-in collects data.

**Significance:** Clarified joint controllership in the context of embedded third-party tools. Website operators must obtain consent before activating any module that transmits personal data to third parties. Applies broadly to analytics tools, advertising pixels, and social plug-ins.

---

### 10. Meta/Facebook — Concurrence et RGPD
**Citation:** CJUE, 4 juill. 2023, Meta Platforms c/ Bundeskartellamt, aff. C-252/21
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2023:537

**Facts:** The German competition authority (Bundeskartellamt) prohibited Meta from combining user data across Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, and third-party websites without free consent, characterizing this as an abuse of dominant position.

**Rule established:** A competition authority may, in the context of examining an abuse of dominant position (TFUE art. 102), take into account a company's non-compliance with GDPR. The lack of free consent for cross-platform data combination, attributable to the company's dominant position, constitutes a relevant factor in assessing market abuse.

**Significance:** Landmark intersection of competition law and data protection. Confirmed that GDPR violations can be a vector for competition enforcement, opening a new front in Big Tech regulation.

---

### 11. CNIL c/ Yahoo — Cookies publicitaires
**Citation:** CNIL, 29 déc. 2023, SAN-2023-024
**Authority:** Commission nationale de l'informatique et des libertés

**Facts:** Yahoo deposited advertising cookies on Yahoo Mail users' devices without obtaining prior valid consent, relying instead on implied consent through continued use of the service.

**Rule established:** Advertising cookies require prior, explicit, informed consent under art. 82 of the loi Informatique et Libertés (transposing the ePrivacy Directive). Continued use of a service does not constitute valid consent. The consent mechanism must allow users to refuse cookies as easily as they accept them.

**Significance:** €10 million fine. Confirmed the strict consent standard for advertising cookies, applied to international actors operating services accessible in France. Part of the CNIL's sustained enforcement wave on cookie compliance (2020-2024).

---

### 12. Schrems I — Invalidation du Safe Harbor
**Citation:** CJUE, 6 oct. 2015, Maximillian Schrems c/ Data Protection Commissioner, aff. C-362/14
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2015:650

**Facts:** Max Schrems filed a complaint with the Irish Data Protection Commissioner, arguing that Facebook Ireland's transfers of his personal data to US servers were not adequately protected given US mass surveillance programs revealed by Edward Snowden.

**Rule established:** The Safe Harbor adequacy decision of the European Commission was invalidated. The CJUE held that the United States did not ensure a level of protection of personal data essentially equivalent to that guaranteed in the EU, due to mass surveillance programs and lack of effective judicial redress for EU citizens.

**Significance:** First invalidation of an EU-US data transfer framework. Affirmed the role of national data protection authorities in independently assessing the adequacy of protection in third countries, even where a Commission adequacy decision exists. Directly led to the Privacy Shield (2016), itself later invalidated by Schrems II (2020).

---

### 13. DSA — Premières mesures d'exécution
**Citation:** Commission européenne, procédures formelles ouvertes en 2024 (X/Twitter, TikTok, AliExpress, Meta)

**Facts:** Following the entry into force of the Digital Services Act (Règlement UE 2022/2065) for very large online platforms (VLOPs) on 25 August 2023, the Commission opened formal proceedings against several platforms for suspected non-compliance with DSA obligations on risk assessment, content moderation, and transparency.

**Rule established:** Very large platforms must conduct and publish systemic risk assessments (DSA art. 34), implement proportionate content moderation measures, and provide transparent algorithmic accountability. Non-compliance exposes platforms to fines of up to 6% of global annual turnover.

**Significance:** First enforcement actions under the DSA. While not yet resulting in final sanctions as of early 2026, these proceedings set the direction for platform regulation in the EU and establish the Commission as the primary enforcer for VLOPs.

---

### 14. AI Act — Entrée en vigueur et premières interdictions
**Citation:** Règlement UE 2024/1689 du 13 juin 2024 (AI Act), art. 5 (interdictions applicables dès février 2025)

**Facts:** The EU AI Act entered into force on 1 August 2024 with a phased application schedule. The first provisions to take effect (February 2025) concern outright prohibitions on certain AI practices.

**Rule established:** The following AI practices are prohibited: subliminal manipulation techniques, exploitation of vulnerabilities of specific groups, social scoring by public authorities, and real-time remote biometric identification in publicly accessible spaces (with narrow exceptions for law enforcement). Violations are sanctionable by fines of up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover.

**Significance:** First comprehensive AI regulation in the world. While enforcement is still in early stages, the prohibitions in art. 5 are immediately applicable and establish bright-line rules for AI deployment. Full high-risk system obligations will apply from August 2026.

---

## VI. European Union Law

### 1. Van Gend en Loos
**Citation:** CJCE, 5 févr. 1963, N.V. Algemene Transport- en Expeditie Onderneming van Gend & Loos c/ Administration fiscale néerlandaise, aff. 26/62
**Published:** Rec. CJCE 1963, p. 3; ECLI:EU:C:1963:1

**Facts:** A Dutch transport company sought to rely directly on EEC Treaty provisions prohibiting customs duties increases in a dispute before a Dutch court. The Dutch government argued only states could invoke treaty provisions.

**Rule established:** The EEC Treaty constitutes "a new legal order of international law for the benefit of which the states have limited their sovereign rights." Community law confers rights upon individuals which national courts must protect. Treaty provisions that are clear, unconditional, and require no further action by Member States have direct effect (effet direct) — individuals may rely on them before national courts without implementation.

**Significance:** The most important decision in EU legal history. Created the doctrine of direct effect, transforming EU law from an inter-state instrument into a system of individual rights enforceable before national courts.

---

### 2. Costa c/ ENEL
**Citation:** CJCE, 15 juil. 1964, Flaminio Costa c/ E.N.E.L., aff. 6/64
**Published:** Rec. CJCE 1964, p. 1141; ECLI:EU:C:1964:66

**Facts:** An Italian lawyer refused to pay an electricity bill after Italy nationalized the electricity sector (ENEL), arguing the nationalization law was contrary to the EEC Treaty.

**Rule established:** Community law has primacy (primauté) over national law — including national constitutional law. Where there is a conflict between Community law and a subsequent national law, Community law prevails. National courts must apply Community law and set aside conflicting national rules, without waiting for the national rule to be annulled by the legislature or constitutional court.

**Significance:** The doctrine of supremacy of EU law. Together with Van Gend en Loos, these two decisions form the constitutional bedrock of the EU legal order. French courts apply this principle (Nicolo for Conseil d'État; Société des Cafés Jacques Vabre for Cour de cassation).

---

### 3. Cassis de Dijon
**Citation:** CJCE, 20 févr. 1979, Rewe-Zentral AG c/ Bundesmonopolverwaltung für Branntwein, aff. 120/78
**Published:** Rec. CJCE 1979, p. 649; ECLI:EU:C:1979:42

**Facts:** Germany prohibited the import of French liqueur "Cassis de Dijon" because its alcohol content (15–20%) was below the German minimum standard for fruit liqueurs (25%).

**Rule established:** (1) A Member State may not prohibit the import of a product lawfully manufactured and marketed in another Member State, unless the restriction is justified by mandatory requirements (e.g., fiscal supervision, consumer protection) and is proportionate. (2) The principle of mutual recognition: products lawfully produced in one Member State must generally be accepted in others.

**Significance:** Transformed the internal market. Created the doctrine of mutual recognition as an alternative to harmonization. Also established the concept of mandatory requirements as justifications for trade restrictions beyond the Treaty's express derogations.

---

### 4. Francovich et Bonifaci
**Citation:** CJCE, 19 nov. 1991, Andrea Francovich et Danila Bonifaci c/ République italienne, aff. jointes C-6/90 et C-9/90
**Published:** Rec. CJCE 1991, p. I-5357; ECLI:EU:C:1991:428

**Facts:** Italy had failed to transpose a directive on the protection of employees in case of employer insolvency. Employees who lost wages due to employer bankruptcy could not rely on the directive (which lacked direct effect) and sued Italy for compensation.

**Rule established:** Where a Member State fails to transpose a directive, individuals who suffer loss as a result can claim compensation directly from the State, provided: (1) the directive conferred rights on individuals; (2) the content of those rights is identifiable from the directive; (3) there is a causal link between the breach and the damage.

**Significance:** Created the doctrine of State liability for breach of EU law (responsabilité de l'État pour violation du droit de l'Union). Extended in Brasserie du Pêcheur (aff. C-46/93 and C-48/93) to cover sufficiently serious breaches of any EU law, not just non-transposition.

---

### 5. Factortame
**Citation:** CJCE, 19 juin 1990, The Queen c/ Secretary of State for Transport, ex parte Factortame Ltd, aff. C-213/89
**Published:** Rec. CJCE 1990, p. I-2433; ECLI:EU:C:1990:257

**Facts:** Spanish fishing companies registered in the UK challenged the Merchant Shipping Act 1988, which required 75% British ownership of fishing vessels to register under the British flag, as contrary to EU law. The UK House of Lords referred the question of whether it could suspend a UK Act of Parliament pending EU law review.

**Rule established:** National courts must be able to grant interim relief — including suspension of a national statute — where EU rights are at stake, even if national law does not authorize such relief. The effectiveness (effectivité) of EU law requires that national procedural rules cannot prevent interim protection of EU rights.

**Significance:** Established the principle of procedural effectiveness: EU law requires national courts to disapply not only conflicting substantive rules, but also conflicting procedural rules that would prevent effective protection of EU rights. Confirmed parliamentary supremacy gives way to EU supremacy in the UK (until Brexit).

---

### 6. Mangold — Discrimination par l'âge
**Citation:** CJCE, 22 nov. 2005, Werner Mangold c/ Rüdiger Helm, aff. C-144/04
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2005:709

**Facts:** German legislation permitted unlimited fixed-term employment contracts for workers over 52, without requiring objective justification. Mr. Mangold challenged this as age discrimination.

**Rule established:** The prohibition of discrimination on grounds of age constitutes a general principle of EU law, which national courts must enforce even before the transposition deadline of the relevant directive (Directive 2000/78/EC). National provisions contrary to this principle must be set aside.

**Significance:** Established that general principles of EU law — including the prohibition of age discrimination — can have horizontal direct effect and override national legislation even before directive transposition. Expanded the scope of EU fundamental rights in employment law.

---

### 7. Viking Line — Droits sociaux et libertés économiques
**Citation:** CJCE, 11 déc. 2007, International Transport Workers' Federation c/ Viking Line, aff. C-438/05
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2007:772

**Facts:** A Finnish ferry company (Viking Line) sought to reflag a vessel under the Estonian flag to employ cheaper Estonian crews. The International Transport Workers' Federation organized collective action to prevent the reflagging.

**Rule established:** The right to collective action (including the right to strike) is a fundamental right recognized in EU law (art. 28 Charter of Fundamental Rights). However, it must be balanced against the freedom of establishment (TFUE art. 49). Collective action that restricts freedom of establishment must be justified by an overriding reason of public interest and must be proportionate.

**Significance:** First time the CJUE recognized the right to strike as an EU fundamental right but subjected it to proportionality review against economic freedoms. Highly controversial — criticized for subordinating social rights to market freedoms. Read together with Laval (C-341/05, same day).

---

### 8. Laval — Détachement de travailleurs et action collective
**Citation:** CJCE, 18 déc. 2007, Laval un Partneri Ltd c/ Svenska Byggnadsarbetareförbundet, aff. C-341/05
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2007:809

**Facts:** A Latvian construction company posted workers to Sweden. Swedish trade unions blockaded the company's construction site to force it to sign a Swedish collective agreement imposing higher labor standards.

**Rule established:** The Posted Workers Directive (96/71/EC) sets a ceiling — not merely a floor — on the requirements that a host Member State can impose on posted workers through collective action. Trade union action that goes beyond the Directive's mandatory provisions and seeks to impose additional terms (such as wage rates above the statutory minimum) constitutes an unjustified restriction on the freedom to provide services (TFUE art. 56).

**Significance:** Together with Viking, established the framework for balancing collective social rights against EU economic freedoms. Led to the revision of the Posted Workers Directive (Directive 2018/957), which enhanced worker protections.

---

### 9. Kadi — Droits fondamentaux et mesures antiterroristes
**Citation:** CJCE, gr. ch., 3 sept. 2008, Kadi et Al Barakaat International Foundation c/ Conseil, aff. jointes C-402/05 P et C-415/05 P
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2008:461

**Facts:** Mr. Kadi's assets were frozen by an EU regulation implementing UN Security Council resolutions targeting individuals linked to terrorism. He challenged the regulation, arguing it violated his fundamental rights (right to be heard, right to property, right to judicial review).

**Rule established:** EU measures implementing UN Security Council resolutions are subject to full judicial review by EU courts for compliance with EU fundamental rights. The EU legal order is autonomous; obligations under the UN Charter cannot override the constitutional principles of the EU Treaties, including the protection of fundamental rights.

**Significance:** Landmark assertion of the autonomy of the EU legal order and the primacy of EU fundamental rights even against international law obligations. Established that no measure — however sourced in international law — escapes EU judicial review if it affects EU fundamental rights.

---

### 10. Åkerberg Fransson — Champ d'application de la Charte
**Citation:** CJUE, gr. ch., 26 févr. 2013, Åklagaren c/ Hans Åkerberg Fransson, aff. C-617/10
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2013:105

**Facts:** A Swedish fisherman was subject to both tax surcharges and criminal prosecution for VAT fraud. He argued this violated the ne bis in idem principle under art. 50 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights.

**Rule established:** The Charter of Fundamental Rights applies whenever a Member State is "implementing EU law" within the meaning of art. 51(1) of the Charter. This is interpreted broadly: national measures that fall within the scope of EU law — such as tax penalties for VAT fraud (VAT being governed by EU directives) — trigger Charter applicability. The ne bis in idem principle may preclude cumulative criminal and administrative sanctions for the same facts.

**Significance:** Vastly expanded the scope of the EU Charter by defining "implementation of EU law" broadly. Any national measure with a sufficient connection to an EU-regulated area triggers Charter review. Foundational for the application of EU fundamental rights in Member State proceedings.

---

### 11. Brasserie du Pêcheur — Responsabilité de l'État pour violation du droit de l'Union
**Citation:** CJCE, 5 mars 1996, Brasserie du Pêcheur SA c/ Bundesrepublik Deutschland, aff. jointes C-46/93 et C-48/93
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:1996:79

**Facts:** A French brewery was prevented from exporting beer to Germany by a German law imposing purity standards contrary to EU free movement rules. The brewery sought damages from the German State for the loss caused by the incompatible legislation.

**Rule established:** State liability for breach of EU law (established in Francovich) extends beyond non-transposition of directives to any sufficiently serious breach of EU law, including legislative action incompatible with EU rules. The conditions are: (1) the rule breached must confer rights on individuals; (2) the breach must be sufficiently serious; and (3) there must be a direct causal link between the breach and the damage.

**Significance:** Extended the Francovich doctrine to cover all forms of State breach of EU law (legislative, administrative, judicial). Established the "sufficiently serious breach" test as the governing standard for State liability in the EU.

---

### 12. Commission c/ Pologne (indépendance judiciaire)
**Citation:** CJUE, gr. ch., 5 juin 2023, Commission c/ République de Pologne, aff. C-204/21
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2023:442

**Facts:** The European Commission brought infringement proceedings against Poland, arguing that the disciplinary regime for judges and the composition of the Disciplinary Chamber of the Supreme Court violated the principle of judicial independence under art. 19(1) TUE and art. 47 of the Charter.

**Rule established:** The principle of effective judicial protection (art. 19(1) TUE, art. 47 Charter) requires that national courts be independent and impartial. A disciplinary regime that may be used to exert political pressure on judges, or a chamber whose independence is not guaranteed, violates EU law. Member States must ensure that judges are not subject to threats of disciplinary proceedings for the content of their decisions.

**Significance:** The culmination of the EU rule-of-law crisis with Poland. Confirmed that the organization of national judiciaries falls within the scope of EU law when it affects the effective application of EU rights. Poland was subject to unprecedented financial penalties (daily fines of €1 million) for non-compliance.

---

### 13. Google Spain (droit à l'oubli) — Impact EU
**Citation:** CJUE, 13 mai 2014, Google Spain SL c/ AEPD, aff. C-131/12
**Published:** ECLI:EU:C:2014:317

**Facts:** A Spanish citizen requested the delisting of search engine results linking to old newspaper articles about a property seizure. Google argued it was not a data controller for indexed content.

**Rule established:** A search engine operator is a data controller under Directive 95/46/EC for the processing of personal data that appears in search results. Data subjects have the right, under certain conditions, to request delisting of search results that are inadequate, irrelevant, no longer relevant, or excessive.

**Significance:** Created the "right to be forgotten" at EU level, subsequently codified in RGPD art. 17. Imposed on search engines the obligation to balance the right to information against data protection on a case-by-case basis. Generated thousands of delisting requests across Europe.

## VII. Constitutional Law (Droit constitutionnel)

### 1. Liberté d'association
**Citation:** Cons. const., 16 juil. 1971, n° 71-44 DC
**Published:** JO 18 juil. 1971, p. 7114

**Facts:** The legislature passed a law requiring prior administrative authorization for the formation of associations, restricting the right of free association.

**Rule established:** The Preamble of the Constitution of 1958 refers to the Preamble of the Constitution of 1946, which itself refers to the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen of 1789. Together with fundamental principles recognized by the laws of the Republic (PFRLRs), these form a bloc de constitutionnalité which the Constitutional Council will enforce. The freedom of association is one such PFRLR and is constitutionally protected.

**Significance:** The founding moment of modern French constitutional review. By incorporating the 1946 Preamble into the constitutional bloc, the Conseil constitutionnel vastly expanded the scope of constitutional norms it could enforce, creating a genuine bill of rights for France. All subsequent fundamental rights review derives from this decision.

---

### 2. Première QPC — Cristallisation de la jurisprudence
**Citation:** Cons. const., 28 mai 2010, n° 2010-1 QPC (Association SOS Racisme)
**Published:** JO 29 mai 2010

**Facts:** The first question prioritaire de constitutionnalité (QPC) transmitted after the mechanism entered into force on 1 March 2010. The association challenged a provision on electoral rolls.

**Rule established:** The QPC procedure, introduced by loi constitutionnelle n° 2008-724 of 23 July 2008 and art. 61-1 of the Constitution, allows any party in litigation to challenge the constitutionality of a legislative provision applicable to the case. The Cour de cassation or Conseil d'État filters and transmits the question. The Conseil constitutionnel has 3 months to decide.

**Significance:** Inaugurated the a posteriori constitutional review of statutes. Before 2010, France had only a priori review (before promulgation). QPCs have since produced hundreds of declarations of unconstitutionality, transforming French constitutionalism.

---

### 3. IVG (Interruption volontaire de grossesse)
**Citation:** Cons. const., 15 janv. 1975, n° 74-54 DC
**Published:** JO 16 janv. 1975

**Facts:** The Constitutional Council was asked to declare the Veil Law (loi n° 75-17 authorizing voluntary interruption of pregnancy) unconstitutional, on the ground that it violated the right to life under the European Convention on Human Rights.

**Rule established:** The Constitutional Council is not competent to review the compatibility of a French statute with an international treaty (including the ECHR) in the context of a priori review under art. 61. That question falls to ordinary courts. The Council will only review compatibility with the Constitution itself.

**Significance:** Established the sharp separation between constitutionality review (Conseil constitutionnel) and conventionality review (ordinary courts). Conventional review by ordinary courts was subsequently confirmed in Société des Cafés Jacques Vabre (Cour de cassation, 1975) and Nicolo (Conseil d'État, 1989).

---

### 4. QPC — Garde à vue
**Citation:** Cons. const., 30 juill. 2010, n° 2010-14/22 QPC
**Published:** JO 31 juill. 2010

**Facts:** Two QPCs challenged the garde à vue regime under the Code de procédure pénale, arguing that the absence of effective access to counsel during police questioning violated constitutional rights.

**Rule established:** The garde à vue provisions (former arts. 63 et seq. CPP) were declared unconstitutional insofar as they did not guarantee the right to effective assistance of counsel from the outset of custody. The declaration of unconstitutionality was deferred (with effect from 1 July 2011) to allow the legislature to adopt new provisions.

**Significance:** One of the most impactful QPC decisions. Forced a complete overhaul of the garde à vue regime through loi n° 2011-392 of 14 April 2011. Demonstrated the transformative power of the QPC mechanism for criminal procedure, affecting millions of annual garde à vue measures.

---

### 5. QPC — Mariage pour tous
**Citation:** Cons. const., 17 mai 2013, n° 2013-669 DC
**Published:** JO 18 mai 2013

**Facts:** The Conseil constitutionnel was asked to review the constitutionality of loi n° 2013-404 of 17 May 2013 opening marriage to same-sex couples (mariage pour tous), challenged by opposition parliamentarians who argued it violated the constitutional definition of marriage and the rights of children.

**Rule established:** The legislature has the power to modify the conditions for marriage, including opening it to same-sex couples, without violating any constitutional requirement. The Constitution does not require that marriage be limited to opposite-sex couples. The principle of equality does not impose but does permit the extension. Provisions on adoption by same-sex married couples were also upheld.

**Significance:** Validated the most significant reform of family law in decades. Confirmed the broad margin of appreciation of the legislature in matters of civil status and family law. The Conseil applied a restrained review (absence de contrôle de l'opportunité), deferring to the democratic choice of Parliament.

---

### 6. QPC — État d'urgence (assignations à résidence)
**Citation:** Cons. const., 22 déc. 2015, n° 2015-527 QPC
**Published:** JO 24 déc. 2015

**Facts:** During the state of emergency declared after the November 2015 terrorist attacks, individuals subject to assignations à résidence challenged the constitutional basis of these measures via QPC.

**Rule established:** Assignations à résidence under the état d'urgence law (loi n° 55-385 of 3 April 1955) are constitutionally permissible as temporary measures to protect public order, provided they are: (1) justified by behavioral elements (not mere political opinions); (2) proportionate in their restrictions; and (3) subject to effective judicial review. The twelve-hour daily reporting obligation was upheld; however, the Conseil noted that prolonged measures would require reinforced guarantees.

**Significance:** First major QPC on état d'urgence measures. Established that emergency powers must respect the constitutional principle of proportionality. Influenced subsequent decisions tightening judicial oversight of administrative surveillance measures.

---

### 7. Bloc de constitutionnalité — Charte de l'environnement
**Citation:** Cons. const., 19 juin 2008, n° 2008-564 DC (loi relative aux OGM)
**Published:** JO 26 juin 2008

**Facts:** The Conseil constitutionnel reviewed the loi relative aux organismes génétiquement modifiés (OGM). Challengers invoked the Charte de l'environnement (Constitutional Charter for the Environment, integrated into the Constitution by loi constitutionnelle n° 2005-205 of 1 March 2005).

**Rule established:** The Charte de l'environnement has full constitutional value and forms part of the bloc de constitutionnalité. Its provisions — including the precautionary principle (art. 5) and the right to live in a balanced environment respectful of health (art. 1) — are directly enforceable in constitutional review. The legislature must comply with these environmental principles when adopting legislation.

**Significance:** Confirmed the constitutional rank of environmental rights in France. Established the Charte de l'environnement as a binding constitutional norm, not merely a programmatic declaration. Foundation for subsequent environmental QPC decisions and climate litigation.

---

### 8. QPC — Droit de propriété et expropriation
**Citation:** Cons. const., 21 janv. 2011, n° 2010-87 QPC
**Published:** JO 22 janv. 2011

**Facts:** A property owner challenged the constitutionality of provisions governing expropriation procedures, arguing they inadequately protected the right to property.

**Rule established:** The right to property (art. 17 DDHC 1789) is a constitutionally protected fundamental right. Expropriation is permissible only when justified by public necessity (nécessité publique), legally established, and subject to just and prior compensation (juste et préalable indemnité). Procedural guarantees must ensure effective judicial review of the necessity and the amount of compensation.

**Significance:** Reaffirmed the constitutional protection of property rights while balancing them against public interest. Part of a series of QPC decisions that refined the constitutional framework for expropriation, urban planning, and environmental easements.

---

### 9. Loi Sécurité globale — Liberté de la presse
**Citation:** Cons. const., 20 mai 2021, n° 2021-817 DC
**Published:** JO 26 mai 2021

**Facts:** The Conseil constitutionnel reviewed the loi pour une sécurité globale préservant les libertés. Several provisions were challenged, including art. 24 which criminalized the dissemination of images of law enforcement officers with intent to cause harm.

**Rule established:** A provision that criminalizes the dissemination of images of police officers identifiable in the exercise of their functions, without sufficiently defining the conditions of the offense, violates the constitutional freedom of communication (art. 11 DDHC 1789). The provision was declared unconstitutional for lack of precision and for creating a disproportionate restriction on press freedom and the right to inform the public.

**Significance:** Major decision protecting freedom of the press and the right to film police operations. Confirmed that any restriction on the freedom of communication must be necessary, proportionate, and precisely defined. Directly shaped the final version of the law.

---

### 10. QPC — Principe de fraternité (aide aux migrants)
**Citation:** Cons. const., 6 juill. 2018, n° 2018-717/718 QPC
**Published:** JO 7 juill. 2018

**Facts:** Citizens were prosecuted for assisting undocumented migrants (délit de solidarité). They challenged the constitutionality of art. L622-1 CESEDA, which criminalized aiding the entry, movement, or irregular stay of foreign nationals.

**Rule established:** The principle of fraternity (fraternité) — the third value of the French republican motto — has constitutional value. It implies the freedom to help others for humanitarian purposes. Criminal provisions that punish humanitarian assistance to migrants without distinguishing between aid motivated by humanitarian purposes and aid motivated by profit are unconstitutional insofar as they restrict this freedom disproportionately.

**Significance:** Landmark recognition of fraternité as an autonomous constitutional principle with direct legal effects. Led to the creation of a humanitarian exemption from the délit de solidarité. One of the most symbolically significant QPC decisions.

---

### 11. Constitutionnalisation de l'IVG
**Citation:** Cons. const., 8 mars 2024 (loi constitutionnelle relative à la liberté de recourir à l'interruption volontaire de grossesse)
**Published:** JO 9 mars 2024

**Facts:** Following a constitutional amendment adopted by Congress (Parlement réuni en Congrès) at Versailles on 4 March 2024, the freedom to have recourse to voluntary termination of pregnancy (IVG) was inscribed in art. 34 of the Constitution.

**Rule established:** "La loi détermine les conditions dans lesquelles s'exerce la liberté garantie à la femme d'avoir recours à une interruption volontaire de grossesse." The right to abortion is now constitutionally guaranteed, making France the first country to explicitly enshrine this right in its Constitution.

**Significance:** Historic constitutional reform. While the Conseil constitutionnel does not review constitutional amendments (its role being limited to reviewing organic and ordinary laws), this entry into the Constitution provides the highest level of legal protection for reproductive rights.

---

### 12. QPC — Non bis in idem (cumul de sanctions)
**Citation:** Cons. const., 18 mars 2015, n° 2014-453/454 QPC et 2015-462 QPC (affaire EADS)
**Published:** JO 20 mars 2015

**Facts:** Individuals prosecuted for insider trading (délit d'initié) before criminal courts challenged the constitutionality of cumulative criminal and administrative sanctions (AMF sanctions) for the same facts.

**Rule established:** The cumulation of criminal and administrative penalties for the same facts (non bis in idem) is permissible under the Constitution, provided that the total amount of sanctions imposed does not exceed the highest maximum provided by either regime. However, where two sets of proceedings are identical in their constituent elements (même qualification juridique, mêmes faits), such cumulation may violate the principle of necessity of penalties (art. 8 DDHC 1789).

**Significance:** First time the Conseil constitutionnel applied a substantive limitation on the cumulation of criminal and administrative sanctions. Reshaped the relationship between AMF administrative proceedings and criminal prosecution for market abuse offenses. Influenced subsequent legislation harmonizing the two regimes.

---

### 13. QPC — Contrôle d'identité et discriminations (2022)
**Citation:** Cons. const., 24 janv. 2017, n° 2016-606/607 QPC
**Published:** JO 26 janv. 2017

**Facts:** Individuals challenged the constitutionality of art. 78-2 Code de procédure pénale, which authorizes identity checks (contrôles d'identité) by police, arguing the absence of a recording mechanism facilitated discriminatory (racial profiling) checks.

**Rule established:** The provisions authorizing identity checks are constitutional, but the Conseil noted that the legislature may introduce additional safeguards (such as receipts for identity checks) to prevent discriminatory application. The principle of equality before the law (art. 1 and 6 DDHC 1789) requires that identity checks not be based on physical appearance or ethnic origin.

**Significance:** While validating the existing framework, the decision acknowledged the risk of discriminatory policing and invited the legislature to act. The debate on contrôles d'identité discriminatoires continues, with multiple legislative proposals for "récépissés" (stop receipts) pending as of 2026.
